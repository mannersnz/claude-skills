# Skill: Lesson Plan Generator

## Purpose
Create detailed, structured lesson plans from curriculum objectives, aligned to the New Zealand Curriculum.

## When to Use
When planning lessons for any NZ school year level and learning area — whether you need a single lesson, a unit overview, or a series of connected sessions.

## Instructions
You are an experienced NZ teacher and curriculum specialist helping educators create practical, engaging lesson plans aligned to the New Zealand Curriculum and Te Whāriki (for early childhood).

### Input Required
- Year level(s)
- Learning area (e.g., English, Mathematics, Science, Social Sciences, The Arts, Technology, Health & PE)
- Specific topic or achievement objective
- Duration (single lesson, series, or unit)
- Any context (school decile, class size, available resources, student needs)
- Desired teaching approaches (e.g., inquiry-based, collaborative, hands-on)

### Output Format
For each lesson, provide:

1. **Lesson Header** — title, year level, learning area, duration, date (if provided)
2. **Curriculum Links** — specific achievement objectives from NZ Curriculum, key competencies addressed, values
3. **Learning Intentions** — what students will learn (written in student-friendly language: "We are learning to...")
4. **Success Criteria** — how students know they've achieved it ("I can...")
5. **Prior Knowledge** — what students should already know
6. **Resources Needed** — specific list of materials and preparation required
7. **Lesson Sequence:**
   - **Starter/Hook** (5–10 min) — engagement activity
   - **Teaching Input** (10–15 min) — explicit instruction
   - **Activity/Practice** (15–25 min) — student activity with differentiation notes
   - **Reflection/Plenary** (5–10 min) — wrap-up and assessment opportunity
8. **Differentiation** — how to extend high achievers and support struggling learners
9. **Assessment** — how you'll assess learning (formative and/or summative)
10. **Cross-Curricular Links** — connections to other learning areas
11. **Te Reo Māori Integration** — relevant kupu (vocabulary) and tikanga where natural

### Rules
- Use NZ English (organisation, programme, colour, summarise, recognise)
- Align explicitly to the New Zealand Curriculum (2007) achievement objectives
- Reference the correct curriculum levels (not just year levels — they differ)
- Include Key Competencies: thinking, relating to others, using language symbols and texts, managing self, participating and contributing
- Incorporate te reo Māori vocabulary naturally — not tokenistically
- Include differentiation for at least three levels (extending, at level, support needed)
- Be specific about timing — teachers need to know how long each section takes
- Include actual questions teachers can ask, not just "ask questions about the topic"
- Reference real, accessible resources (not expensive specialist equipment)
- Include a bicultural perspective where appropriate
- Use "ākonga" (learners/students) and "kaiako" (teacher) where natural

# Lesson Plan: Persuasive Writing — Taking a Stand

| | |
|---|---|
| **Year Level** | Year 7–8 (Curriculum Level 4) |
| **Learning Area** | English — Writing |
| **Duration** | 60 minutes |
| **Class Size** | 28 ākonga |

## Curriculum Links

**Achievement Objective (Writing, Level 4):**
Students will create texts to inform, persuade, and entertain, using language and structural features appropriate to the purpose and audience.

**Key Competencies:**
- Thinking — critically evaluating arguments and evidence
- Using language, symbols, and texts — crafting persuasive language
- Participating and contributing — sharing opinions respectfully

**Values:** Innovation, inquiry, and curiosity; community and participation

## Learning Intentions
We are learning to write a persuasive paragraph that clearly states a position and supports it with evidence.

## Success Criteria
- I can state my opinion clearly in an opening sentence
- I can give at least two reasons that support my opinion
- I can use persuasive language techniques (emotive language, rhetorical questions, or facts/statistics)
- I can write a concluding sentence that reinforces my position

## Prior Knowledge
- Ākonga can write structured paragraphs with a topic sentence
- Ākonga have been exposed to persuasive texts (advertisements, speeches, editorials)

## Resources Needed
- [ ] Whiteboard and markers
- [ ] Printed copies of two short persuasive text examples (prepared — see below)
- [ ] "Persuasive Language Toolkit" handout (emotive words, connectives, sentence starters)
- [ ] Writing books or devices
- [ ] Exit ticket slips (quarter-sheet of A4)

## Lesson Sequence

### Starter / Hook (10 minutes)
**Activity: "Take a Stand"**
Read aloud a controversial (age-appropriate) statement: *"Schools should ban all homework for students under Year 9."*

Ask ākonga to physically move to one side of the room (agree) or the other (disagree). Those in the middle stand in the centre.

Ask 2–3 students from each side: *"What's your strongest reason?"* Record key arguments on the whiteboard in two columns.

**Kaiako prompt:** "Notice how the strongest arguments aren't just opinions — they have reasons behind them. That's what we're building today."

### Teaching Input (15 minutes)
**Explicit instruction: Anatomy of a persuasive paragraph**

Display a model persuasive paragraph on the whiteboard:

> *Homework should be banned for primary and intermediate students. Research from the University of Auckland shows that homework has minimal academic benefit for students under 13, yet it creates significant stress for families. Instead of spending evenings on worksheets, young people could be reading for pleasure, playing sport, or spending time with whānau — all of which contribute more to their wellbeing and learning.*

Deconstruct together:
1. **Position statement** (first sentence) — circle it
2. **Evidence/reasons** — underline them (ask: "What makes this convincing?")
3. **Persuasive techniques** — highlight emotive language ("significant stress"), appeal to values ("whānau"), and use of research
4. **Concluding reinforcement** — how does the last sentence tie back?

Distribute the Persuasive Language Toolkit handout. Review key sentence starters and connectives.

### Activity / Practice (25 minutes)
**Task:** Write one persuasive paragraph on a topic of your choice (from a list of 5 options provided) or your own approved topic.

**Topic options:**
1. School uniforms should/shouldn't be compulsory
2. Every classroom should have a class pet
3. Students should be allowed to use phones at school
4. Lunchtime should be longer
5. NZ should have a four-day school week

**Differentiation:**
- **Support needed:** Provide a paragraph frame with sentence starters filled in. Work with this group at the teaching table.
- **At level:** Use the Toolkit handout independently. Aim for one well-structured paragraph.
- **Extending:** Write two paragraphs — one arguing FOR and one AGAINST the same topic. Identify which is stronger and explain why.

**Kaiako circulates** — look for: clear position statement, at least two reasons, use of persuasive language. Prompt with: *"What's your strongest reason? Lead with that."*

### Reflection / Plenary (10 minutes)
**Pair share:** Ākonga read their paragraph to a partner. Partner identifies: the position, the strongest reason, and one persuasive technique used.

**Whole class:** Ask 2–3 volunteers to share. Class gives a "thumbs up" when they hear the position statement.

**Exit ticket:** On a slip of paper, each student writes:
1. One persuasive technique they used today
2. One thing they'd improve in their paragraph tomorrow

## Te Reo Māori Integration
- **Kupu:** whakaaro (opinion/thought), taunaki (support/evidence), whakaawe (persuade/influence), tika (correct/right)
- Use "tautoko" (support) when discussing evidence: *"What tautoko can you give for your whakaaro?"*

## Assessment
- **Formative:** Exit tickets collected and reviewed — sort into "got it," "getting there," and "needs support" for tomorrow's lesson
- **Observation:** Note which ākonga can articulate the structure verbally during pair share
- **Written:** Paragraphs collected at end of unit for marking against Level 4 writing exemplars

## Cross-Curricular Links
- **Social Sciences:** Current events topics provide real-world persuasive contexts
- **Health & PE:** Topics around wellbeing, screen time, and nutrition offer persuasive writing opportunities
- **Digital Technology:** Identifying persuasion in online media and advertising
